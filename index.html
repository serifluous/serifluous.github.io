Case study: Mobile app dashboard redesign

LendUp provides loans and credit cards to customers who are often denied access to credit by traditional banks. Driven by a social mission to improve people’s financial health, LendUp builds products with financial education, transparency, and dignity in mind.

I led the design of the credit card mobile app. I worked alongside a product manager, copywriter, and front-end engineer, and checked in with the design team.

The problem

Our support team regularly received calls with basic questions about customers’ balance, credit, and the payment process. These calls drove about 30% of our total call volume.

After digging into these calls, we knew there were improvements we could make in the usability of the app dashboard, the first screen customers landed on.

Goals

At a high-level, our goals were to

  • Decrease the number of customer calls about known issues, including the lack of clarity around available credit, credit line, and payment information.
  • Discover and address usability issues given time and resource constraints, which meant we were limited in considering a broader redesign.

Research & testing

From past research, we already knew that our customers needed to know their available credit more immediately than their current balance, and also often wanted to know their credit line. Since our customers were credit-constrained, the amount of credit they had was always top of mind.

To discover any unknown issues, we ran our first usability tests on the app and also found that people

  • Didn’t want their credit score to be prominent for privacy and emotional reasons
  • Were intrigued but weren’t sure why we showed credit utilization
  • Couldn’t figure out how to contact support but would instinctively scroll to find information they couldn’t immediately find
  • Weren’t always sure if a payment was due
  • Often had to squint or bring their phone up close to read text in the app

The findings confirmed much of what we knew—that our information hierarchy was unclear, that our app needed to suit audiences with different vision abilities—but also uncovered much we didn’t know, especially around the emotional experience of checking your account information.

The proposal

The framework for better considered hierarchy

For this project, we adopted Jason Fried’s article the Obvious, the Easy, and the Possible (OEP) as a framework for hierarchy discussions with stakeholders. This helped direct conversations that could’ve derailed with a comment like, “Can you make the font bigger?” to more productive discussions around what information to prioritize.

Instead of discussing isolated components, we were now discussing trade-offs.

From this framework and our research, I landed on a hierarchy for the mobile app dashboard.

Making it Obvious

Larger type, more linear information flow

The dashboard now prioritizes the information that customers needed most immediately: available credit, minimum payment information, and making a payment. Information that was less desired upfront was moved into a tabbed carousel.

More discoverable contact and settings entry points

Customers can get to their settings and our contact form on the dashboard instead of having to guess at whether it might be revealed by a side menu icon.

A simplified payments card

We moved unnecessary payment information off the main dashboard and onto a separate payments screen to keep the payments card as obvious as possible. I worked with our copywriter to humanize the language around making payments.

Modular card UI system

This project was a testing ground for our new card component system. I developed a modular system we could use for a variety of flows and information types so we could scale our UI and front-end efficiency with new product features.

New visual identity and style guides

LendUp had adopted a new visual identity, and the mobile app was the first customer servicing touchpoint designed to reflect the new brand.

Releasing the new dashboard

As much upfront testing helps, releasing the new dashboard to the public meant there were still issues to be discovered. Thankfully, we had the opportunity to iterate and refine by:

  • Doing a cool thing
  • Doing another cool thing
  • Doing something else

Results

Since launch:

  • We’ve cut balance and payment inquiry call volume by about 38%.
  • We ran usability testing again, and participants found available credit, payment, and contact information faster than in previous tests.
  • Customers have shown more focused interaction with the dashboard on the actions we want them to tap on.

The Arrow Card app is available now on the App Store and Play Store
