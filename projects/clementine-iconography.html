Clementine Iconography

Goal

Revamping Clementine’s iconography started out as a simple project to soften corners and unify stroke widths on the app’s tab bar:

However, I knew that simply updating the tab bar wouldn’t be enough. We needed a complete overhaul of our iconography, and I chose three words I wanted to communicate that I believe reflect Clementine’s product and brand: trustworthy, friendly, and secure.

Several product sprints had resulted in bloat, with icons of different styles cobbled together each time a new feature required them. A new icon set could bring visual consistency throughout the entire app and promote a greater sense of calm and reassurance in our customers.

Keeping this in mind, I aimed for an icon set style that would be personable but not excessively informal.

“Competitor” Analysis

Typically, at the beginning of a project I’d take a look at products in the same space as Clementine and use their designs as a launch point to figure out what other enterprise products are doing well (or not).

However, given that the visual tone of enterprise design is traditionally unenthusiastic, I took a broader view of the landscape. I looked, especially, at products that:

  1. Were used for communication or productivity.
  2. Relied on icons to represent a wide range of concepts and actions.
  • Airbnb
  • Dropbox
  • Foursquare + Swarm
  • Shopify
  • Slack
  • Sunrise

I also bucketed a few icon sets on Dribbble for inspiration.

Sketching

Sketching icons with pen and paper allowed me to work more quickly in exploring ideas than if I had dived straight into vectors in Sketch.

A few icons I had trouble with:

  • Copy contact info to clipboard—how do I distinguish this from the share icon?
  • Conferences—the team and I were still deciding on what constituted a conference (Is it scheduled? Is it a multi-person call?) and how it differed from a normal call.
  • Directory & Groups—How do I make these as gender-neutral or as representative of all genders as possible?
  • Invite via email/contacts/text—how do I distinguish this from our normal email, contacts, and text message icons?

Mockups

After some vector work in Sketch, I assembled the redesigned icons into their appropriate screens by overlaying them on app screenshots. This made it easy to get feedback from the team since everything was contextualized with real content. The mockups made it clear exactly where the icons and app needed visual and functional improvement.

For example, the conference scheduling screen (below) had icons in almost every entry field. This cluttered a screen that already forced a large amount of cognitive load on the user because of the multiple form actions required. Moreover, the icons didn’t add much utility that the text labels couldn’t already provide.

I also iterated a great deal on icons for groups, empty states, and invite buttons since they were challenging in terms of inclusion, tone, and increasing motivation toward an action (respectively). I focused, especially, on using the right visual tone, while keeping icons as simple, consistent, and meaningful as possible.

Prototyping

Prototyping was minimal since icons within the app are pretty static. I did do some quick animations in Pixate for some features (e.g., inviting contacts, call quality ratings, onboarding), but mostly because I was designing them simultaneously with the icon redesign.

Final Design

The final icon set used only Clementine’s primary orange and grays (except in the rare cases of red delete buttons). The app’s previous icons ventured into our secondary color scheme and relied too much on those colors to provide visual contrast with little meaning inherent to the icons themselves.

I kept stroke widths and corner radii as uniform as possible across all icons on all screens to keep visual consistency throughout the app. As I was redesigning the icons, other members of the Clementine team were building out a universal navigation menu (which I also created icons for), and it was great seeing consistency championed in all aspects of the app.

In total, I created a set of over 100 icons, as well as more for the desktop app.

Next Steps & Lessons Learned

We heard back from our customers, who were overwhelmingly positive in their feedback. They said that the latest Clementine release with the new icons completely changed the look of the app (for good), which was exactly what I was going for.

Along the way, I also

  • Created a new file organization in Dropbox for design assets, screenshots, and specs to help our engineers build faster. They love it, and it’s also improved the time it takes for the design team to find and share assets. A little organizational work goes a long way.
  • Effectively made the three brand words I relied on at the start of the project (trustworthy, friendly, secure) a key part of our product. These words were the way we began presenting our product to Clementine’s different audiences.

Starting out, I wasn’t experienced at all with creating icons, or even working much with vectors. There are definitely things I would change with how I structured the icons’ strokes (Is it a centered stroke? Inside? Outside?) and making layer names as clean and specific as possible. Things like this come back to haunt me when we need to edit and use the icons for other purposes (like an icon background for onboarding).

Now that the Clementine team’s settled on the differences between conferences and calls, and the information and feature architecture of the app has changed as a result, the icons related to those features could use some additional care.
